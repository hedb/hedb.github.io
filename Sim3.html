Is the Moon There When Nobody Looks?

The question that deeply unsettled Einstein about quantum mechanics as it began to formalize in the early 20th century was not merely about the intricacies of particles and waves; it was fundamentally about the nature of reality itself.

What perturbed Einstein was the absence of a complete, concrete reality in the theoretical framework provided by quantum mechanics. According to this theory, particularly as interpreted through the Copenhagen Interpretation, there isn't a "hidden" reality that we simply haven't observed yet. Instead, this reality doesn't actually exist until we look at it.

The most famous thought experiment to grapple with this conceptual quagmire is Schrödinger's Cat. In this paradox, a cat in a sealed box is both alive and dead until someone opens the box to observe it. This challenges our everyday understanding of reality and prompts the question: Are there really grown men and women in the field of physics who believe that only when we open the box does something actually happen to the cat?

Unsatisfied and even a bit incredulous about such a perspective, Einstein set forth to expose what he viewed as the absurdity of this idea. His skepticism ultimately led to the influential EPR paper, which questioned the completeness and logical consistency of quantum mechanics.
